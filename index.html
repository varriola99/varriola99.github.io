How Spending Impacts Performance in the Premier League¶

How does spending impact the ranking of a team in the Premier League?

Introduction¶

The soccer industry has been booming over the past couple of years. The sports current operating income is averaged out to be 84 million US Dollars, which is the highest it has ever been. With teams like Manchester United valued to be 4.12 billion US Dollars, its not hard to see how such teams have the potential to succeed. Teams get money from ticket sales, merchandise sales, sponsorships, ownership, television deals, and other forms of publicity. Teams with the biggest fanbase make the most income which they can spend on bringing in new and better players that help the team with the top of their league. Money is the biggest factor in creating some of the best soccer teams in the world. But one team defied the odds.

During the 2015-2016 season, Leicester City F.C. achieved what is now considered one of the greatest sporting stories of all time. Leicester City F.C., who used to be a part of the EFL Championship, the second-highest division of the English football league system, worked their way up and got promoted to the Premier League, the highest division in the English football league, and proceed to win the league all within 2 seasons. Leicester City F.C. spent approximately 27,300,000 US Dollars on transferring in players. That same season, Manchester United spent 222,000,000 US Dollars on transfers and got 5th place on the Premier League table standings.

Using data from the transfer market relating to the Premier League, our group will collect, tidy, and examine this data to analyze the impact of money and spending has on a soccer teams placement in the Premier League. We will then use this data to then predict how teams could rank in the league table based on their spendings. We hope that our readers, both unfamiliar and familiar to the sport, find this tutorial intriguing as we delve into another side of soccer not usually broadcasted, the side that now matters just as much as the game itself.

Upon trying to retrieve the data, we realized that the original data we are trying to work with was being pulled from multiple sites. Helper Functions¶

We create some helper functions so that graphing the top and bottom 5 teams is easier.

Our first helper funtion converts a string value into a numerical value. This is done as the monetary values that we use from the data we collected uses "K" and "m" to symbolize thousands and millions of US Dollars. We also go ahead and make all nonexistent values, marked by "0" or "-" in the data table, equal to 0. This will allow for easy graphing as we are now only working with numerical values.

In [ ]:
# Convert the string value into a numerical value
def actualVal(string):
    
    if (string == "0" or string == "-"):
        return 0
    else:
        
        string = string[1:]
        
        if string[len(string) - 1] == "k":
            return float(string[: len(string) - 1]) * 1000
            
        elif string[len(string) - 1] == "m":
            return float(string[: len(string) - 1]) * 1000000
        
        return 0

Prediction Analysis¶

Taking a look at our predictions, it is evident the effect that money is had over time in the league when given context. Early on all across Europe, teams were less willing to spend on international players. They would rather promote homegrown talent in their teams. Because of this, teams spent less. Spending did not have as big of an effect on the table as other factors, such as having a good youth academy. This can be seen in the first few grouping of our predictions graph, as the y-axis shows the difference between the bigger spenders and the lower spenders is smaller than 1 in most cases. In the past 10 - 15 years though, the bigger teams have been greatly outspending the smaller teams for the best international talent. This spending to gain higher positions in the table, which was seen in the plotting of the highest and lowest spenders, can be seen with the predictions graph as well. The y-axis shows that margin between bigger spenders and lower spenders grows significantly, which the difference being more than 5 spots in some of the groupings.

Conclusion¶

While money might not be the only reason that a team may do well, it is increasingly showing up that spending more money than your opposition is going to win you games. Leicester City was a very rare case in modern soccer, which created a thrilling season for fans around the world. With the money they had invested, it was not predicted that they would make it in the top half of the table. This, combined with the fact that it was only their second season after being promoted to the top flight of English soccer, makes for one of the greatest stories in the game.

The league in the past few seasons has seen unbelievable amounts of spending. It creates the debate whether this spending is technically fair for all of the teams in the league, as many teams who do not earn enough often fall to the bottom of the table. Specifically, the bottom three teams in the table who end up thrown into the division below through relegation. It is still a controversial topic in the league, but the work here sheds light on the definite impact cause by money in the modern game.
